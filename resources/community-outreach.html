Hospital Discharge Units — Your #1 Referral Source

Highest-Value Relationship

A single hospital discharge planner might refer 5–10 clients per month. This is the most important relationship you can build.

How to identify the right contact

  • Call the hospital switchboard
  • Ask for: "Discharge planning team" or "Patient flow coordinator" or "Social work department"
  • Some hospitals call it "Transitional care" or "Hospital in the Home coordination"
  • Large hospitals have dedicated discharge planners for aged care patients

First visit approach

  1. Call ahead to introduce yourself and ask when's a good time to visit
  2. Bring: Co-branded brochures (10+), a box of quality chocolates (Cadbury Favourites, Lindt — this genuinely works)
  3. What to say: "I'm [Name], a care coordinator with Trilogy Care. I help patients transitioning home from hospital get the home care services they need — everything from personal care to nursing to physio. I'd love to be on your referral list."
  4. Leave your card and brochures
  5. Ask: "What's the best way for you to send me a referral?"

Building the ongoing relationship

Relationship Rules

Visit monthly — same day each month if possible

Bring something small each time (morning tea, chocolates)

Ask how their week has been — be a human, not just a business contact

Close the loop on referrals: "Just wanted to let you know Mrs Smith is settled and her services are all set up. Thanks for the referral."

Be responsive — when they call, answer or call back within the hour

GP Practices

How GPs refer to aged care

GPs don't usually refer directly — the practice manager or practice nurse is your key contact. They handle referrals and can recommend you to patients asking about home care.

Approach

  1. Visit the practice (without appointment is fine for leaving materials)
  2. Ask to speak with the practice manager
  3. Introduce yourself: "I coordinate home care services for people over 65 in [suburb]. Can I leave some information?"
  4. Leave 10 brochures at reception
  5. Follow up in 4 weeks

What to leave at reception

  • Co-branded brochures (in a neat stack or stand)
  • Your business card
  • A one-page flyer: "Are your patients getting the most from their Support at Home funding?"

Allied Health Home-Visit Practices

Physios, OTs, and podiatrists who do home visits are gold. They see clients at home and can spot when someone needs more care support.

Why they're valuable

  • They visit clients at home — they see the reality of someone's living situation
  • They build trust over time with their patients
  • They naturally have conversations about care needs
  • Many already work across NDIS and aged care

Retirement Villages and Lifestyle Communities

The opportunity

Retirement villages (Ingenia, HomeTown, Aveo, Lendlease, local independents) are full of people in the aged care demographic. Village managers are your gateway.

Village manager introduction

Script — Village Manager

Call or visit the village office:

"I'm [Name], I coordinate home care services for older Australians in the area. Would it be possible to leave some information for your residents, or perhaps host a short information session about Support at Home?"

Most village managers welcome this — it adds value for their residents.

Hosting a talk at a village

  • Duration: 20–30 minutes + Q&A
  • Best time: Mid-morning (10am), mid-week
  • Content: "Getting the Most from Your Support at Home Funding" (see Client Acquisition Playbook for talk template)
  • Bring: Brochures, a sign-up sheet for "free care assessment chat"
  • Follow up: Call everyone on the sign-up sheet within 3 days

Noticeboard materials

If the village has a noticeboard, ask permission to place:

  • Your co-branded brochure
  • A simple A4 flyer: "Support at Home — Are you getting the most from your home care funding? Free consultation with [Your Name]. Call [number]."

RSLs and Community Clubs

Approach

  • Contact the sub-branch secretary or club manager
  • Ask about noticeboard placement and event opportunities
  • Offer to sponsor a morning tea or social event (cost: $50–100 for catering)
  • These clubs have strong community networks — word of mouth is powerful
Pro Tip

RSL clubs and bowling clubs often have large senior memberships. A $75 morning tea can put you in front of 30–50 potential clients in a single session. Bring brochures and a sign-up sheet.

Local Council and Community Centres

Seniors programs coordinators

Every local council has a community services team. Contact them and ask about:

  • Seniors groups and programs
  • CALD (culturally and linguistically diverse) community groups
  • Carer support groups
  • Council-run seniors events and expos

CALD communities

Many culturally diverse communities have their own seniors groups, community centres, and leaders. Approach with cultural sensitivity and offer to present through a community leader.

Cultural Sensitivity

When engaging with CALD communities, always work through a trusted community leader. Offer translated materials if possible. Ask your Partnership Manager about Trilogy's multilingual resources.

Tracking Your Relationships — CRM-Lite

You don't need expensive software. Keep a simple spreadsheet of your referral contacts and review it monthly.

Monthly review checklist

  • Who is sending referrals? Nurture those relationships first.
  • Who hasn't been visited recently? Schedule a visit this week.
  • Where are the gaps in your network? Identify new contacts to approach.
  • Are brochures stocked at all locations? Replenish as needed.
  • Have you followed up on every referral with the referrer? Close the loop.

Your Outreach Target

Month 1: Identify and visit 3 hospitals, 5 GP practices, 2 retirement villages

Month 2: Add allied health contacts, RSLs, and community groups

Month 3+: Maintain monthly cadence. Aim for 10+ active referral relationships

Remember: Consistency beats intensity. A monthly visit is worth more than a one-off blitz.
