NDIS-to-Aged-Care Skills Map

8 min read

Your NDIS Skills Are Directly Transferable

There is a widespread belief in the aged care industry that NDIS and aged care require entirely different skill sets. This is incorrect. The coordination work is structurally similar. The regulatory burden in aged care is substantially lower. And the human skills — complex case management, family communication, multi-agency navigation — are identical.

"Nothing in aged care is harder than NDIS."

Andrew Wallace — Trilogy Care. Said to every NDIS coordinator considering the move into aged care.

The table below maps every major NDIS competency to its aged care equivalent, with a complexity rating to show whether the aged care equivalent is simpler, similar, or simply different. In almost every case: it's simpler.

The Compliance Comparison

Compliance is the most common anxiety for NDIS coordinators considering aged care. The reality should put that anxiety to rest.

📝
NDIS audit: 18-month cycles, registration renewal, evidence portfolios, independent auditors on-site
✅
Aged care review: Annual standards check, approximately 45 minutes, managed by Trilogy Care with coordinator documentation
📄
NDIS: Coordinator maintains registration, insurance, NDIS Worker Screening, individual compliance obligations
🎯
Aged care: Trilogy Care holds the provider registration. Future Focus coordinators operate under Trilogy's compliance umbrella.
The key insight

In aged care, Trilogy Care carries the compliance burden — provider registration, quality standards, audits, billing compliance, and government reporting. Future Focus coordinators focus on coordination and client relationships. That is a fundamentally different risk and workload profile to NDIS.

What Is Actually Different

Honest differences, because they exist and coordinators should know them going in.

Area NDIS Aged Care (Support at Home)
Client cohort Primarily working-age adults; often lifelong relationships 65+ adults; often a shorter but intensive relationship period
Emotional content Disability identity, advocacy, rights-based conversations Ageing, loss of independence, end-of-life planning; grief often present
Funding model NDIS plan; needs-based, individualised, disability-specific Support at Home budget; entitlement-based, acuity-classified
Advocacy Active advocacy often required in plan reviews Less adversarial; advocacy through care planning rather than review contestation
Clinical complexity Varies widely; some participants have complex medical needs Often high clinical need; Trilogy's nursing team supports escalation
Preparing for the emotional difference

The most significant adjustment for NDIS coordinators is the emotional tenor of aged care conversations — clients are often navigating loss of independence, fear of residential care, and end-of-life matters. This is not harder than NDIS. But it is different. Jake Kelly-Grubb provides mentoring on this during coordinator induction.

The Verdict

Future Focus coordinators have already demonstrated they can manage some of the most complex care coordination work in Australia. The NDIS system — with its multiple funding bodies, contested reviews, behaviour support requirements, and 18-month audit cycles — is genuinely harder than Support at Home coordination.

Aged care adds emotional complexity and a different client cohort. It removes most of the system complexity. With Trilogy Care handling compliance, billing, and platform administration, Future Focus coordinators can do what they are already very good at: building trust with families and managing care well.
